UX/UI DESIGN – CASE STUDY


San Francisco Elections

An upgraded interface for San Franciscans to easily register and learn how to vote.



Big Idea

We prototyped a mobile and desktop redesign of the San Francisco Department of Elections site to encourage young San Franciscans to educate themselves and register to vote before the November ‘20 Presidential Election.

↗︎ Case Study Deck – take a closer look at our process.

Overview

Role – UX/UI Designer, Visual Designer
Team – Johnson Aderohunmu, Kendra McIntyre, Yuxi Shang
Tools – Figma, Google Suite, Miro, Procreate, Trello
Time – July ‘20 (3 weeks)







Problem

The current site is too content-dense.

Completing simple tasks (i.e. finding a polling place or viewing election results) involve too many clicks and take too much time. These inefficiencies coupled with the outdated interface turns away new users looking to register and learn how to vote.



– A DESIGN CHALLENGE EMERGED –

How might we improve the interface to increase voter turnout amongst San Francisco’s youth?



Solution

We introduced a standardized UI system and improved information architecture with card-based design for users to take action quickly and find information efficiently.

In order to appeal to a young voter audience, we incorporated bold, emotional imagery that invoke a sense of urgency and importance for understanding today’s political climate.

Therefore, only about 32% of San Francisco’s eligible young voters are registered to vote.



User Persona

After researching why the young vote is lacking, we approached this project to encourage new voters to register and get involved – especially during an election year. Here we have Justin, freshly out of university looking to kick off his independent adult life – starting with voting for his future.





Usability Tests

ORIGINAL SITE

We asked 4 users to complete each of these 4 tasks on the original website:

  1. Register to vote / Check if already registered
  2. Find the nearest polling place
  3. Learn how to vote-by-mail
  4. Check most recent election results


Due to COVID-19 and safe-distancing rules, explaining how to successfully vote-by-mail became a top priority. To ensure that San Franciscans can exercise their right to vote in a safe manner during the November election, the Department of Elections agreed to mail ballots to all registered voters in October.



RESULTS

Users took, on average, a little over 2 minutes to complete each task. Our users found it difficult to find what they were specifically looking for, ultimately needing clearer calls to action on the home page.

Professional Looking

100% of our users felt the original site looked professional and corresponded with their experiences with other government sites.

Cluttered and busy

75% of our users felt it looked too busy and overwhelming with information and text, pointing out the lack of visual aids of images and symbols.

Failed to find polling place

32% of our users failed to receive the correct address of their polling place and complained about the navigation bar and dropdowns.



Site Map

A main improvement was simplifying and shortening the site map and navifation bar. There is too much information displayed on this single site, bluring tasks and confusing users.

The original site’s top navigation bar has 7 options, with an average of 6 dropdowns each. For our redesign, we narrowed it down to 4 options with 4 dropdowns each.





Lo-Fi

After receiving usability feedback on the original site, I sketched wireframes focusing on card-based design to simplify the site’s information architecture. We wireframed mobile screens first to work from micro to macro before designing the desktop version.





Visual Design

Our color inspiration comes from the City of San Francisco seal, We brightened the colors and selected the blue as a primary color.

The blue served as a patriotic color symbolizing trust and loyalty. Accents of red, yellow, and green helped focus our user’s attention to specific areas of the screen.

Using a sans-serif font with wider kerning for the body helped with user readability. We coupled it with a serif font for headers to keep the site’s govern ment officiality.

We also kept design consistency with our icons by using only an outlined style from Google’s Material Design.





Imagery

While iterating wireframes, I suggested we use black and white photography to incite a serious and emotional response from our users. Today’s political climate is especially polarizing and to use trendy illustrations felt childish and belittling.

Instead, we incoporated red and blue illustrations as hover states over black and white images to still feel youthful and metropolitan.



Final Design

We kept in mind three goals we generated from the research results for the final design. These goals helped drive my design decisions.

GOALS

  1. Add easy-to-click calls to action
  2. Simplify top navigation bar / hamburger menu
  3. Lower number of clicks / duration of time to complete tasks



Navigation

FEATURE

I designed our navigation dropdowns with icons and text captions for easy readability and accessibility. Users will have a better idea about the page before having to click on it first.



Find Polling Place

FEATURE

Users can find their polling place by using the navigation bar or finding the call to action on the home page. Users can enter their home address and click submit, and the associated polling place address will appear with a map and directions.



Vote-by-Mail

FEATURE

With COVID-19 and safe-distancing rules, it is likely that users will mail in their votes rather than vote at polling stations with unappealing long lines and foot traffic. Users can rely on the navigation bar or find the call to action on the home page to learn how to vote-by-mail.



Takeaways

It was satisfying working to solve a problem so relevant in my own life. Similar to our user persona, I opted out of voting in the '16 Presidential Election, but now understand the importance of voting as a civic duty.

I enjoyed studying and implementing micro-interactions throughout the site. We were able to track how they help the user click through the site and follow a user journey. In the future I'd like to do more usability testing while timing task completion to make the process of understanding voting information more accessible and digestible for all our users.
